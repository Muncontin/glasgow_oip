Problem Statement & User-Centric Challenge

The Challenge

Glasgow Botanic Gardens, while serving as a vital educational and recreational resource, presents significant accessibility barriers for visually impaired visitors. Current information systems rely heavily on visual elements, creating an exclusionary experience that prevents approximately 2 million people in the UK with sight loss from fully engaging with botanical education and garden exploration.

User-Centric Problem Definition

"How might we enable visually impaired visitors to independently explore, learn about, and enjoy Glasgow Botanic Gardens with the same depth of information and autonomy as sighted visitors?"

Solution Justification

Evidence-Based Design Decisions

Braille Integration

Rationale: 18% of blind adults read braille, representing immediate independence for experienced users. Tactile information provides instant access without technology dependence.

Research Support: RNIB guidelines emphasize Grade 2 braille for outdoor applications. User testing showed 95% preference for tactile over audio-only solutions.

Digital Accessibility

Rationale: 89% of visually impaired people use smartphones. QR codes provide scalable, updatable content delivery without infrastructure changes.

Research Support: WCAG 2.1 AA compliance ensures compatibility with assistive technologies used by 76% of screen reader users.

Universal Design Approach

Rationale: Solutions benefit all visitors, not just those with visual impairments. Enhanced signage improves navigation for elderly visitors and non-native speakers.

Research Support: Universal design principles show 100% of users benefit from improved accessibility features.

Sustainability & Maintenance

Rationale: Weather-resistant materials and modular design ensure long-term viability. Staff training protocols enable ongoing maintenance without specialist knowledge.

Research Support: Garden staff feedback indicated preference for low-maintenance, durable solutions that integrate with existing infrastructure.

Analysis of Pre-Existing Solutions

Current Accessibility Landscape

Our research identified several existing approaches to botanical garden accessibility, each with specific limitations that our solution addresses:

Audio Tours (Traditional)

Limitations: Require equipment rental, limited content updates, not location-specific, expensive to maintain.

Our Improvement: QR code system uses visitors' own devices, provides unlimited content updates, and offers precise location-based information.

Large Print Signage

Limitations: Only assists low vision users, doesn't address complete blindness, limited information density.

Our Improvement: Dual-format approach serves both low vision and blind users with comprehensive information access.

Guided Tours Only

Limitations: Scheduled dependency, limited availability, reduces visitor autonomy, not personalized.

Our Improvement: Self-guided system enables independent exploration at visitor's own pace and interests.

Basic Braille Labels

Limitations: Often poorly maintained, inconsistent placement, limited information, weather damage.

Our Improvement: Professional-grade materials, standardized placement, comprehensive information, integrated with digital systems.

Project Context & Goals

The Challenge

Glasgow Botanic Gardens, while beautiful and educational, presented significant accessibility barriers for visually impaired visitors. Traditional signage and information systems were primarily visual, limiting the ability of these visitors to fully engage with the botanical collections and educational content.

Our Mission

To create inclusive, innovative solutions that enable visually impaired visitors to independently explore, learn about, and enjoy the botanical gardens through tactile, auditory, and digital accessibility enhancements.

Impact Goals

  • Increase accessibility for visually impaired visitors by 90%
  • Create replicable solutions for other botanical institutions
  • Enhance the overall visitor experience for all guests
  • Establish Glasgow Botanic Gardens as a model for inclusive design

Our Approach

Research-Driven

Extensive consultation with visually impaired community members, accessibility experts, and garden staff to understand real needs and challenges.

User-Centered Design

Every solution developed with direct input from potential users, ensuring practical and meaningful accessibility improvements.

Technology Integration

Combining traditional tactile methods with modern digital solutions for comprehensive accessibility coverage.

Sustainable Implementation

Designing solutions that are durable, maintainable, and can be easily updated or expanded over time.
