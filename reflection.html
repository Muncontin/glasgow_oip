Project Reflection & Critical Analysis

A comprehensive reflection on our design journey, partner interactions, achievements, and areas for improvement in creating accessible botanical experiences.

Reflection on Partner Interactions

Glasgow Botanic Gardens Partnership

What Worked Well

Throughout the project, we collaborated with our assigned partner, Chrissy, and attended a guided tour conducted by Elspeth. Both were incredibly warm, welcoming, and supportive of our initiative to enhance accessibility for visually and audibly impaired visitors. Their openness made communication smooth and fostered a collaborative atmosphere from the beginning.

The guided tour with Elspeth was particularly valuable. It allowed us to observe the garden's layout and signage firsthand and to experience how information is typically presented to visitors. We discovered that the guided tour provided significantly more context and depth than the available signage, highlighting the importance of interactive, human-led experiences in a setting like the Botanic Gardens.

Additionally, our informal discussion with Elspeth after the tour gave us unique insights into how visually impaired visitors are sometimes guided through more tactile experiences, such as being allowed to touch plants and sculptures under supervision. These authentic interactions helped us better understand the user experience and the garden's current limitations. Furthermore, with Elspeth's input as someone who was partially blind herself, we were able to develop our ideas into the final prototypes with her suggestions on how to improve the tactile experience for visually impaired visitors.

Challenges and Learning Opportunities

One of the primary challenges we encountered was the constraint of limited funding. Through conversations with Chrissy, she revealed that the gardens were reliant on a public funding and volunteer model, which forced us to respect tight budgets and limited technical support. This meant that any solution we proposed needed to be low-cost and realistic for implementation, pushing us to think more critically and creatively within those boundaries.

Another learning point came during our evaluation of our early idea: implementing a braille pathway. While initially promising, we found this to be impractical due to the narrow pathways within the garden, the high costs associated with such an infrastructure change, and the potential negative impact on the soil quality, which could affect the health of the plants. This forced us to reconsider our assumptions and pivot toward more feasible, non-invasive alternatives.

We also came to realize that there is currently minimal formal support for blind and deaf visitors. There is currently no braille signage, no audio description system, and only optional training were provided to volunteers to support the needs of visually impaired visitors. While this revealed a gap in accessibility, it also clarified the opportunity and necessity for targeted, cost-effective interventions.

Impact on Solution Development

Our interaction with the partners had a direct and meaningful impact on the development of our solution. The initial meeting with Chrissy helped set realistic expectations about the budget and current state of accessibility within the gardens, guiding us to explore simpler, scalable interventions. This understanding led us to move away from complex infrastructural ideas and instead focus on adaptable, low-cost solutions like braille-enhanced signage with QR Code that directs to an enhanced website with BSL Translations.

Additionally, Chrissy's answers to our questions during the Q&A session showed that since most volunteers were not technologically competent and were not trained to support the needs of visually or audibly impaired visitors, the Botanic Gardens had limited resources to implement significant changes. This insight helped us to reshape our initial solutions to focus on ideas that did not require any training or extensive technical support so that they could be easily adopted by the existing volunteers.

Furthermore, observing the guided tour and learning about the informal tactile experiences such as getting the visitors to touch the veins and the sample plants provided to visually impaired visitors inspired us to enhance our prototype and come up with the idea of our Braille Guidebook. These real-world insights helped us refine our direction and iterate our ideas with user experience at the core.

Overall, the partnership helped us root our project in the actual needs and limitations of the space, ensuring that our solution was not only innovative but also practical, inclusive, and implementable.

What We Are Proud Of

Grounded, Empathetic Research

We’re proud of how we took the time to truly understand the environment we were designing for. By attending guided tours, observing accessibility issues firsthand, and engaging with both Chrissy and Elspeth, we made sure our ideas were based on real experiences, not assumptions.

Effective Stakeholder Collaborations

We had meaningful interactions with our partners Chrissy and Elspeth, who generously shared their insights with us. Their support and feedback were instrumental in helping us shape a solution that fits the Botanic Gardens' context.

Willingness to Pivot and Adapt

When we realised that our original idea of the braille pathway wasn’t practical due to space and budget constraints, we didn’t hesitate to rethink our approach. We adapted quickly and refocused our efforts on ideas that were both impactful and feasible.

Designing Within Real Constraints

We embraced the challenge of designing within the real-world limitations of the garden, such as limited funding and infrastructure. This pushed us to be more creative and come up with solutions that are low-cost, implementable, and sustainable.

Prioritising Inclusive Impact

We’re proud that our solution doesn’t just benefit people with visual or auditory impairments but it’s designed with everyone in mind. By taking a universal design approach, we aimed to improve the visitor experience for elderly guests, non-native speakers, and anyone who might need extra support navigating the space.

Collaborative Team Dynamics

One of the things we’re most proud of is how well we worked together as a team. From the very beginning, we clearly defined our roles and responsibilities, which helped ensure that tasks were managed efficiently and communication remained open. Despite having different working styles and ideas, we created a space where everyone felt heard and respected, and this made a big difference in how we approached problem-solving.

During key phases like the stakeholder meetings, guided tours, and prototype development, we collaborated closely to document findings, share insights, and bounce ideas off each other. For example, after our meeting with Chrissy, we had an in-depth team discussion to reassess the feasibility of our original idea, the braille pathway and together we agreed to pivot toward more achievable solutions. This collective decision-making process showed us how valuable it is to stay flexible and support one another through uncertainty.

We also made sure that everyone contributed to the design iterations, research, and field observations, which gave us a more well-rounded understanding of the problem and helped shape a more inclusive and thoughtful solution. The diversity of perspectives within our team ultimately strengthened the outcome of our work.

Areas for Improvement and Learning

Making Prototyping More Tangible

Throughout the project, all of our prototypes were kept in digital format. While this was efficient for iteration and presentation, we believe that developing physical versions of certain ideas such as the braille signboard or the tactile guidebook could have added significant value. Tangible prototypes would have allowed stakeholders, especially those unfamiliar with accessibility tools, to better grasp the practicality and impact of our solutions.

Limited Engagement with End Users

Due to the short timeframe of the project, we were unfortunately unable to directly engage with visually or audibly impaired individuals. While we gained valuable insights through our interactions with stakeholders and guided tours, we recognise that including direct input from affected users would have further strengthened our understanding of their specific needs. This limitation highlighted the importance of co-design and inclusive consultation, which we would prioritise more fully in a longer-term or future project.

More Structured Documentation

Our team collected a lot of valuable insights from the tour, interviews, and observations, but we could have improved the way we organised and recorded this information. A more structured documentation process, such as a shared insights log or accessibility audit checklist would have made it easier to refer back to key findings when developing and justifying our solution.

Future Directions and Recommendations

Co-Design with Visually and Audibly Impaired Users

Moving forward, we recommend involving blind and deaf individuals directly in the design process through co-creation workshops, interviews, or usability testing. Their lived experiences are essential for validating assumptions and ensuring that solutions truly meet their needs. While time constraints limited this during our project, it should be a priority in future iterations.
