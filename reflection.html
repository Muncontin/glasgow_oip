Project Reflection & Critical Analysis

A comprehensive reflection on our design journey, partner interactions, achievements, and areas for improvement in creating accessible botanical experiences.

Reflection on Partner Interactions

Glasgow Botanic Gardens Partnership

What Worked Well

Our partnership with Glasgow Botanic Gardens exceeded expectations in several key areas. The garden management demonstrated exceptional openness to accessibility improvements, providing unrestricted access for research and testing phases. Their staff's willingness to participate in interviews and feedback sessions provided invaluable operational insights that shaped our practical implementation approach.

The collaborative approach to problem-solving was particularly effective. Rather than imposing external solutions, we worked closely with garden staff to understand existing workflows, maintenance capabilities, and visitor patterns. This collaborative mindset resulted in solutions that integrate seamlessly with current operations while addressing real accessibility needs.

Challenges and Learning Opportunities

Initial communication challenges arose from different organizational cultures and priorities. The garden staff's primary focus on horticultural excellence sometimes conflicted with accessibility considerations, requiring careful negotiation and compromise. We learned the importance of framing accessibility improvements as enhancements to the overall visitor experience rather than additional burdens.

Budget constraints and approval processes created timeline pressures that required adaptive project management. This taught us valuable lessons about stakeholder management and the importance of building flexibility into project timelines when working with institutional partners.

Impact on Solution Development

Partner feedback directly influenced our material selection, placement strategies, and maintenance protocols. The garden staff's expertise in weather resistance and durability requirements led to significant improvements in our prototype designs. Their insights about visitor behavior patterns informed our information hierarchy and content strategy.

RNIB Scotland Collaboration

Professional Validation and Guidance

The partnership with RNIB Scotland provided crucial professional validation and technical expertise that elevated our solution quality. Their guidance on braille standards, accessibility compliance, and user testing methodologies ensured our solutions met professional accessibility standards rather than well-intentioned but inadequate attempts.

Access to their network of visually impaired volunteers was invaluable for authentic user testing. This connection provided insights that would have been impossible to obtain through secondary research or assumptions about user needs.

Challenges in Coordination

Coordinating schedules with volunteer participants required significant flexibility and patience. The specialized nature of accessibility testing meant that standard user testing protocols needed adaptation, requiring additional time and resources we hadn't initially anticipated.

Balancing professional standards with practical implementation constraints sometimes created tension between ideal accessibility solutions and feasible implementations within our project scope and timeline.

Long-term Relationship Building

This partnership established foundations for ongoing collaboration beyond our project timeline. RNIB Scotland's endorsement provides credibility for future implementation phases and potential expansion to other botanical institutions.

Community Engagement Reflection

User-Centered Approach Success

Direct engagement with visually impaired community members was the most impactful aspect of our research process. Their lived experiences provided insights that transformed our understanding of accessibility challenges and solution requirements. The enthusiasm and constructive feedback from community participants validated our approach and motivated continued refinement.

Representation and Inclusion Challenges

Ensuring diverse representation within our user testing group required intentional effort and multiple recruitment strategies. We learned that accessibility needs vary significantly among individuals with different types and degrees of visual impairment, requiring solutions that accommodate this diversity.

Language barriers and cultural considerations added complexity to community engagement that we hadn't fully anticipated, highlighting the importance of inclusive research methodologies.

What We Are Proud Of

Authentic User-Centered Design

We are most proud of our commitment to authentic user-centered design throughout the entire project lifecycle. Rather than making assumptions about accessibility needs, we invested significant time and effort in building genuine relationships with the visually impaired community. This approach resulted in solutions that address real needs rather than perceived problems.

The depth of our user research, including 12 individual interviews, multiple focus groups, and extensive on-site testing, demonstrates our dedication to understanding the lived experiences of our target users. The positive feedback from community participants validates that our solutions genuinely improve their botanical garden experience.

Technical Innovation and Quality

Our technical achievements represent significant innovation in accessibility technology for outdoor environments. The development of weather-resistant braille materials that maintain tactile sensitivity while withstanding Scottish weather conditions required extensive material research and testing. The integration of QR code technology with offline capabilities addresses real-world connectivity challenges that many accessibility solutions overlook.

The WCAG 2.1 AA compliance of our digital platform ensures compatibility with assistive technologies, demonstrating our commitment to professional accessibility standards. The modular design approach enables future updates and expansions, creating sustainable long-term value.

Collaborative Team Dynamics

Our multidisciplinary team's collaborative approach created synergies that elevated the quality of our solutions beyond what any individual could have achieved. The combination of design expertise, technical skills, accessibility knowledge, and project management created a comprehensive skill set that addressed all aspects of the challenge.

The team's commitment to inclusive decision-making and continuous learning fostered an environment where diverse perspectives were valued and integrated into our solutions. Our 100% attendance rate at daily stand-ups and consistent engagement throughout the project timeline demonstrates exceptional team cohesion.

Systemic Impact Potential

We are proud that our solutions address systemic accessibility barriers rather than providing temporary fixes. The comprehensive three-pillar approach (tactile, digital, and enhanced signage) creates multiple access points that accommodate different user preferences and capabilities.

The replicable nature of our solutions means that other botanical institutions can adapt and implement similar accessibility enhancements, potentially creating widespread positive impact for visually impaired visitors across the UK and beyond.

Professional Recognition and Validation

The endorsement from RNIB Scotland and positive feedback from Glasgow Botanic Gardens management validates the professional quality and practical viability of our solutions. This recognition demonstrates that our work meets professional accessibility standards and addresses real institutional needs.

The interest expressed by other botanical institutions in adopting similar solutions indicates that our work has broader impact potential beyond the initial project scope.

Areas for Improvement and Learning

Project Timeline and Resource Management

While we successfully delivered all project objectives, our initial timeline estimates proved optimistic for several key activities. User testing coordination required more time than anticipated, particularly when working with specialized populations and coordinating multiple stakeholders. Future projects would benefit from more generous timeline buffers for community engagement activities.

Resource allocation could have been more strategic, particularly regarding material testing and prototype development. Earlier investment in professional-grade materials would have accelerated the testing cycle and provided more reliable durability data.

Broader Accessibility Considerations

Our focus on visual impairment, while thorough, meant that other accessibility needs received less attention. Future iterations should consider mobility impairments, cognitive accessibility, and hearing impairments to create truly universal design solutions.

The intersection of multiple accessibility needs within individual users could have been explored more thoroughly. Some visually impaired visitors may also have mobility or cognitive considerations that affect their interaction with our solutions.

Technology Integration Challenges

While our QR code system works effectively, we underestimated the complexity of ensuring consistent performance across different smartphone models and operating systems. More extensive device testing would have identified compatibility issues earlier in the development process.

The offline capability, while functional, could be more robust. Future development should explore progressive web app technologies that provide more seamless offline experiences.

Sustainability and Long-term Planning

While we developed maintenance protocols, the long-term sustainability model could be more comprehensive. Future projects should include detailed financial planning for ongoing maintenance, content updates, and system expansions.

Staff training protocols, while adequate, could be more extensive to ensure consistent quality of accessibility support over time. Regular refresher training and quality assurance processes would maintain solution effectiveness.

Measurement and Evaluation

Our success metrics, while comprehensive, could include more longitudinal data collection to assess long-term impact and user satisfaction. Establishing baseline measurements before implementation would provide clearer evidence of improvement.

The evaluation framework could include more quantitative measures of independence and user confidence, providing clearer evidence of solution effectiveness for future funding and expansion efforts.

Communication and Documentation

While our documentation is comprehensive, earlier investment in professional video documentation would have better captured the user testing process and community feedback. Visual documentation of the design process would enhance the project's educational value for other teams.

More systematic documentation of decision-making rationale would provide valuable insights for future projects and help other teams avoid similar challenges.

Future Directions and Recommendations

Immediate Next Steps

  • Staff training program development and delivery
  • User feedback collection system establishment
  • Maintenance protocol refinement based on real-world usage

Medium-term Expansion

  • Garden-wide implementation following successful pilot
  • Integration with existing garden mobile applications
  • Multi-language content development
  • Partnership development with other botanical institutions

Long-term Vision

  • National network of accessible botanical gardens
  • Technology platform licensing to other institutions
  • Research publication and academic dissemination
  • Policy advocacy for accessibility standards in public gardens
