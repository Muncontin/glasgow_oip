Project Reflection & Critical Analysis

A comprehensive reflection on our design journey, partner interactions, achievements, and areas for improvement in creating accessible botanical experiences.

Reflection on Partner Interactions

Glasgow Botanic Gardens Partnership

What Worked Well

Throughout the project, we collaborated with our assigned partner, Chrissy, and attended a guided tour conducted by Elspeth. Both were incredibly warm, welcoming, and supportive of our initiative to enhance accessibility for visually and audibly impaired visitors. Their openness made communication smooth and fostered a collaborative atmosphere from the beginning.

The guided tour with Elspeth was particularly valuable. It allowed us to observe the garden's layout and signage firsthand and to experience how information is typically presented to visitors. We discovered that the guided tour provided significantly more context and depth than the available signage, highlighting the importance of interactive, human-led experiences in a setting like the Botanic Gardens.

Additionally, our informal discussion with Elspeth after the tour gave us unique insights into how visually impaired visitors are sometimes guided through more tactile experiences, such as being allowed to touch plants and sculptures under supervision. These authentic interactions helped us better understand the user experience and the garden's current limitations. Furthermore, with Elspeth's input as someone who was partially blind herself, we were able to develop our ideas into the final prototypes with her suggestions on how to improve the tactile experience for visually impaired visitors.

Challenges and Learning Opportunities

One of the primary challenges we encountered was the constraint of limited funding. Through conversations with Chrissy, she revealed that the gardens were reliant on a public funding and volunteer model, which forced us to respect tight budgets and limited technical support. This meant that any solution we proposed needed to be low-cost and realistic for implementation, pushing us to think more critically and creatively within those boundaries.

Another learning point came during our evaluation of our early idea: implementing a braille pathway. While initially promising, we found this to be impractical due to the narrow pathways within the garden, the high costs associated with such an infrastructure change, and the potential negative impact on the soil quality, which could affect the health of the plants. This forced us to reconsider our assumptions and pivot toward more feasible, non-invasive alternatives.

We also came to realize that there is currently minimal formal support for blind and deaf visitors. There is currently no braille signage, no audio description system, and only optional training were provided to volunteers to support the needs of visually impaired visitors. While this revealed a gap in accessibility, it also clarified the opportunity and necessity for targeted, cost-effective interventions.

Impact on Solution Development

Our interaction with the partners had a direct and meaningful impact on the development of our solution. The initial meeting with Chrissy helped set realistic expectations about the budget and current state of accessibility within the gardens, guiding us to explore simpler, scalable interventions. This understanding led us to move away from complex infrastructural ideas and instead focus on adaptable, low-cost solutions like braille-enhanced signage with QR Code that directs to an enhanced website with BSL Translations.

Additionally, Chrissy's answers to our questions during the Q&A session showed that since most volunteers were not technologically competent and were not trained to support the needs of visually or audibly impaired visitors, the Botanic Gardens had limited resources to implement significant changes. This insight helped us to reshape our initial solutions to focus on ideas that did not require any training or extensive technical support so that they could be easily adopted by the existing volunteers.

Furthermore, observing the guided tour and learning about the informal tactile experiences such as getting the visitors to touch the veins and the sample plants provided to visually impaired visitors inspired us to enhance our prototype and come up with the idea of our Braille Guidebook. These real-world insights helped us refine our direction and iterate our ideas with user experience at the core.

Overall, the partnership helped us root our project in the actual needs and limitations of the space, ensuring that our solution was not only innovative but also practical, inclusive, and implementable.

Reflection Essay: Glasgow Botanic Gardens Accessibility Project

Throughout the Glasgow Botanic Gardens accessibility project, we were driven by a strong sense of empathy and purpose. Our process was grounded in direct engagement, observational research, and collaborative dialogue, which allowed us to develop context-sensitive solutions that aimed to be inclusive, feasible, and impactful.

One of the values that shaped our work was empathetic research. By attending guided tours and speaking with stakeholders like Chrissy and Elspeth, we ensured that our design decisions were not speculative but informed by lived experiences. This grounding helped us move beyond assumptions and toward solutions that responded to real-world challenges.

Stakeholder collaboration was a cornerstone of our approach. Chrissy and Elspeth’s openness gave us deep insights into the Garden’s operations and constraints. Rather than treating them as passive informants, we embraced them as co-designers, allowing their perspectives to actively shape the direction of our design thinking.

When our initial idea: a braille pathway has proved to be impractical due to spatial and ecological constraints, we demonstrated our willingness to pivot. Instead of viewing this as a failure, we saw it as a growth opportunity. We collectively shifted towards a more viable solution: QR-coded signage and a braille-enhanced guidebook. This flexibility reinforced the importance of staying adaptable and context-aware.

Facing real-world constraints such as limited funding and infrastructure, we took these as creative prompts rather than restrictions. Chrissy’s guidance helped us shape low-cost, sustainable interventions that aligned with the Garden’s capacities. We learned that impactful, inclusive design does not always require major infrastructure.

We also took a universal design approach, ensuring that our solutions didn’t just serve visually or audibly impaired individuals but also supported elderly visitors, non-native speakers, and people with cognitive differences. This broadened our sense of accessibility to one of inclusive usability for all.

Our team dynamics were another source of pride. Early role definition allowed us to manage tasks efficiently while respecting each member’s working style. We created an open, reflective team culture that allowed us to challenge ideas constructively and support one another through uncertainty. Moments like post-meeting debriefs with Chrissy helped us reassess and co-develop better-aligned ideas.

We’re proud that everyone contributed to ideation, research, and fieldwork, resulting in a more thoughtful, well-rounded solution. Through this experience, it wasn’t just our prototype that matured but our own capacity for reflection, empathy, and collaboration.

That said, the project surfaced several areas for improvement. While our digital prototypes were useful for iteration and presentation, we believe that developing physical versions of certain ideas such as the braille signboard or the tactile guidebook could have added significant value. Tangible prototypes would have allowed stakeholders, especially those unfamiliar with accessibility tools, to better grasp the practicality and impact of our solutions.

We also recognise a key limitation in our process: the lack of direct engagement with end users. Although we consulted with stakeholders, we were unable to involve visually or audibly impaired individuals firsthand. This highlighted the critical importance of co-creation and continuous feedback loops - elements we would prioritise in future iterations. Direct input from those with lived experience would have significantly deepened our understanding of their specific needs and enhanced the relevance of our solutions.

Finally, we identified the need for more structured documentation. While we gathered valuable insights through interviews and site visits, we lacked a shared system to consolidate and refer back to our findings. Introducing design logs, decision journals, or audit checklists could improve our process going forward.

Looking ahead, we strongly advocate for deeper integration of co-design principles. Designing alongside blind and deaf individuals through interviews, workshops, and prototype walkthroughs would help surface needs and insights we may have otherwise missed. In a nutshell, this project taught us that external observation is only part of the picture; designing for accessibility demands that lived experience is placed at the heart of the process.

TLDR – Key Takeaways

  • Grounded our ideas in real-world accessibility challenges through stakeholder tours and observations.
  • Treated Chrissy and Elspeth as co-designers, not just informants.
  • Pivoted away from an impractical braille pathway to more scalable solutions like QR signage and tactile guides.
  • Embraced design constraints as creative boundaries rather than limitations.
  • Designed inclusively for a wide range of users, including the elderly and non-native speakers.
  • Fostered strong team collaboration through clear roles, open communication, and mutual reflection.
  • Identified key areas for growth: tangible prototyping, better documentation, and co-creation with end users.
  • Future work must embed lived experiences at the core of accessibility design, not rely solely on external observation.
