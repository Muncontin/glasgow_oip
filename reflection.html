Project Reflection & Critical Analysis

A comprehensive reflection on our design journey, partner interactions, achievements, and areas for improvement in creating accessible botanical experiences.

Reflection on Stakeholder

Stakeholder

With the final solutions completed, we are glad that the 3 week journey is finally over. The various interactions between team members, stakeholders and our peers who helped us to test our solutions helped us to grow as a team and improve our designs over the duration of the module. Firstly, with Chrissy, our partner, and Elspeth, our tour guide of the day at the Botanical Gardens, they helped us to shape the initial solutions as well as understand the different constraints that the team had to work around.

Chrissy's answers to our questions during the Q&A session showed that since most volunteers were not technologically competent and were not trained to support the needs of visually or audibly impaired visitors, the Botanic Gardens had limited resources to implement significant changes. This insight helped us to reshape our initial solutions to focus on ideas that did not require any training or extensive technical support so that they could be easily adopted by the existing volunteers.

Furthermore, she also revealed that the gardens were reliant on a public funding and volunteer model, which forced us to respect tight budgets and limited technical support. This meant that any solution we proposed needed to be low-cost and realistic for implementation, pushing us to think more critically and creatively within those boundaries. This understanding led us to move away from complex infrastructural ideas and instead focus on adaptable, low-cost solutions like braille-enhanced signage with QR Code that directs to an enhanced website with BSL Translations. Additionally, this meant that during our evaluation of our early idea: implementing a braille pathway. While initially promising, we found this to be impractical due to the narrow pathways within the garden, the high costs associated with such an infrastructure change, and the potential negative impact on the soil quality, which could affect the health of the plants. This forced us to reconsider our assumptions and pivot toward more feasible, non-invasive alternatives. Chrissy also revealed that there is currently no support for the visually and audibly impaired visitors.

Elspeth’s replies to our impromptu interview revealed that only optional training was provided to volunteers to support the needs of visually impaired visitors. She also shared that sometimes for visually impaired visitors, they are guided through more tactile experiences, such as being allowed to touch plants and sculptures under supervision. As Elspeth was a partially blind person herself, we were able to develop our ideas into the final prototypes with her suggestions on how to improve the tactile experience for visually impaired visitors and enrich their experience of the gardens as much as possible. This experience inspired us to enhance our prototype and come up with the idea of our Braille Guidebook. These real-world insights helped us refine our direction and iterate our ideas with user experience at the core.

The guided tour conducted by Elspeth also allowed us to observe the garden's layout and signage firsthand and to experience how information is typically presented to visitors. We discovered that the guided tour provided significantly more context and depth than the available signage, highlighting the importance of interactive, human-led experiences in a setting like the Botanic Gardens.

TLDR – Key Takeaways

  • Insights from Chrissy (partner) and Elspeth (tour guide) helped us understand real-world constraints and refine our ideas.
  • Chrissy revealed that most volunteers aren’t trained or tech-savvy, so we focused on solutions that don’t require digital tools or special training.
  • Due to public funding and limited resources, we had to design low-cost, non-invasive solutions which ruled out ideas like a braille pathway.
  • We pivoted to ideas like braille-enhanced signage with QR codes linking to a BSL-accessible website.
  • Elspeth, who is partially blind, shared how tactile guidance (e.g. touching plants/statues) helps blind visitors which inspired our Braille Guidebook.
  • Observing a live tour highlighted how much more informative it is than the existing signs, reinforcing the value of human-led, interactive experiences.

Reflection on Prototyping

Prototyping

Next during the prototyping journey, the team realized that accessibility is often overlooked in design and we made sure to do our best to improve our designs based on what the users might need. Through empathy mapping and informal interviews, we redesigned our prototype based on feedback from users, peers and even our Teaching Assistants to see if our prototype was intuitive and easy to use for our users. We experimented with various methods such as the TalkBack on Android to emulate how the visually impaired would navigate our website.

Testing these prototypes was both enlightening and humbling, where it showed us that even when our team was developing solutions for the target users in mind, there were still many things that we overlooked like button designs or layouts of the braille guidebook that we were unable to notice unless we were placed in similar situations as the users by using blindfolds. And even then because we were the ones working on the solutions, there were many assumptions that we had that were actual issues such as the BSL translation button not being clear about what purpose it serves according to several users.

All these lead to a redesign and overhaul of some of our prototypes with the feedback in mind leading to the current solutions proposed. In the end, our prototype phases didn’t just validate ideas, they reshaped them, reminding us that genuine inclusivity lies in observing, listening, and relentlessly refining until solutions become seamless extensions of the user’s experience.

TLDR – Key Takeaways

  • The team became aware of how easily accessibility is neglected in design and committed to putting user needs at the forefront.
  • We used empathy mapping, informal interviews, and feedback from users, peers, and TAs to test and refine our designs.
  • Using tools like Android’s TalkBack and blindfolds gave us insight into user experiences, but also highlighted that simulations have limits.
  • Despite designing with users in mind, we discovered overlooked problems like unclear button functions and guidebook layout issues.
  • We learned that being the designers introduced bias and assumptions that only real user feedback could challenge.
  • Iterative feedback and testing reshaped our designs significantly, teaching us that true inclusivity requires continuous observation, listening, and refinement.

Reflection on Usability Testing

Usability Testing

Conducting usability tests on our proposed accessibility solutions revealed significant room for improvement. We carried out two separate tests, each targeting a specific user group. The first focused on how effectively deaf users could navigate from physical signage to a website hosting a British Sign Language (BSL) video. To simulate the experience of someone who prefers sign language over written English, we intentionally translated the webpage into a foreign language unfamiliar to all participants. This allowed us to observe whether they could intuitively locate and play the BSL video without relying on written instructions.

The second test assessed the accessibility of our tactile guidebook design. Lacking both access to blind participants and a physical prototype, we created a digital version compatible with screen readers like TalkBack on Android. Participants navigated the guidebook using these tools while keeping their eyes closed, voicing any challenges or positive impressions they experienced.

Designing the guidebook test forced us to think creatively. We had to simulate the experience of a blind user without fully understanding the nuances of visual impairment or braille, despite our prototype relying heavily on tactile and braille-based features. Using screen readers helped bridge the gap caused by the lack of a physical prototype, but we acknowledge that this approach is ultimately a compromise. The depth and accuracy of insights gained cannot replace feedback from actual visually impaired users.

Nonetheless, both tests generated valuable findings. Despite our intention to design accessible solutions, users encountered notable friction. The BSL video button, for instance, was not easily recognisable, even to tech-savvy participants. In the guidebook test, users frequently mistook the instructional text for the actual QR code and struggled to identify its location. This highlighted the importance of spatial clarity and tactile guidance in the physical layout. These issues made us realise that even small design elements can create significant barriers if not approached from the user’s perspective. It challenged our assumptions about what we thought was intuitive and reminded us that accessibility isn’t just about adding features but it’s also about ensuring those features are truly usable by the people they’re meant to help.

Ultimately, the execution and designing of the usability testing deepened our understanding of accessibility challenges. It revealed assumptions we had made unconsciously, and pushed us to prioritize real-world usability over theoretical design. These findings are crucial in refining our solutions to better meet the needs of those with visual and auditory impairments.

TLDR – Key Takeaways

  • Testing revealed that well-intentioned accessibility features can still create barriers if not designed from the user's perspective.
  • We learned that assumptions about what is “intuitive” often don’t hold true for diverse users, especially those with sensory impairments.
  • Simulating accessibility needs by using screen readers or foreign languages helped bridge gaps, but we realised this is not a substitute for real user feedback.
  • The difficulty users had with small design elements like button recognition or QR code placement taught us how clarity and simplicity are in accessible design.
  • The lack of access to blind testers pushed us to think creatively by finding alternative ways to test a tactile design in a non-tactile format, leading us to explore digital screen readers as a workaround
  • We gained a deeper appreciation that accessibility isn’t just about feature inclusion, but about usability and real-world effectiveness.
  • The process made us reflect on the importance of iterative testing and involving users with lived experience to avoid designing based on assumptions.

Reflection Essay: Glasgow Botanic Gardens Accessibility Project

Throughout the Glasgow Botanic Gardens accessibility project, we were driven by a strong sense of empathy and purpose. Our process was grounded in direct engagement, observational research, and collaborative dialogue, which allowed us to develop context-sensitive solutions that aimed to be inclusive, feasible, and impactful.

One of the values that shaped our work was empathetic research. By attending guided tours and speaking with stakeholders like Chrissy and Elspeth, we ensured that our design decisions were not speculative but informed by lived experiences. This grounding helped us move beyond assumptions and toward solutions that responded to real-world challenges.

Stakeholder collaboration was a cornerstone of our approach. Chrissy and Elspeth’s openness gave us deep insights into the Garden’s operations and constraints. Rather than treating them as passive informants, we embraced them as co-designers, allowing their perspectives to actively shape the direction of our design thinking.

When our initial idea: a braille pathway has proved to be impractical due to spatial and ecological constraints, we demonstrated our willingness to pivot. Instead of viewing this as a failure, we saw it as a growth opportunity. We collectively shifted towards a more viable solution: QR-coded signage and a braille-enhanced guidebook. This flexibility reinforced the importance of staying adaptable and context-aware.

Facing real-world constraints such as limited funding and infrastructure, we took these as creative prompts rather than restrictions. Chrissy’s guidance helped us shape low-cost, sustainable interventions that aligned with the Garden’s capacities. We learned that impactful, inclusive design does not always require major infrastructure.

We also took a universal design approach, ensuring that our solutions didn’t just serve visually or audibly impaired individuals but also supported elderly visitors, non-native speakers, and people with cognitive differences. This broadened our sense of accessibility to one of inclusive usability for all.

Our team dynamics were another source of pride. Early role definition allowed us to manage tasks efficiently while respecting each member’s working style. We created an open, reflective team culture that allowed us to challenge ideas constructively and support one another through uncertainty. Moments like post-meeting debriefs with Chrissy helped us reassess and co-develop better-aligned ideas.

We’re proud that everyone contributed to ideation, research, and fieldwork, resulting in a more thoughtful, well-rounded solution. Through this experience, it wasn’t just our prototype that matured but our own capacity for reflection, empathy, and collaboration.

That said, the project surfaced several areas for improvement. While our digital prototypes were useful for iteration and presentation, we believe that developing physical versions of certain ideas such as the braille signboard or the tactile guidebook could have added significant value. Tangible prototypes would have allowed stakeholders, especially those unfamiliar with accessibility tools, to better grasp the practicality and impact of our solutions.

We also recognise a key limitation in our process: the lack of direct engagement with end users. Although we consulted with stakeholders, we were unable to involve visually or audibly impaired individuals firsthand. This highlighted the critical importance of co-creation and continuous feedback loops which we would like to prioritise in future iterations. Direct input from those with lived experience would have significantly deepened our understanding of their specific needs and enhanced the relevance of our solutions.

Finally, we identified the need for more structured documentation. While we gathered valuable insights through interviews and site visits, we lacked a shared system to consolidate and refer back to our findings. Introducing design logs, decision journals, or audit checklists could improve our process going forward.

Looking ahead, we strongly advocate for deeper integration of co-design principles. Designing alongside blind and deaf individuals through interviews, workshops, and prototype walkthroughs would help surface needs and insights we may have otherwise missed. In a nutshell, this project taught us that external observation is only part of the picture; designing for accessibility demands that lived experience is placed at the heart of the process.

TLDR – Key Takeaways

  • Grounded our ideas in real-world accessibility challenges through stakeholder tours and observations.
  • Treated Chrissy and Elspeth as co-designers, not just informants.
  • Pivoted away from an impractical braille pathway to more scalable solutions like QR signage and tactile guides.
  • Embraced design constraints as creative boundaries rather than limitations.
  • Designed inclusively for a wide range of users, including the elderly and non-native speakers.
  • Fostered strong team collaboration through clear roles, open communication, and mutual reflection.
  • Identified key areas for growth: tangible prototyping, better documentation, and co-creation with end users.
  • Future work must embed lived experiences at the core of accessibility design, not rely solely on external observation.
