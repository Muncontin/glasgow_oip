Prototype Solutions

Our team developed and tested multiple prototype solutions to enhance accessibility at Glasgow Botanic Gardens. These prototypes focus on creating inclusive experiences for visually impaired visitors through innovative braille integration and digital accessibility features.

Braille Solution Prototypes

Interactive Braille Guidebook

Prototype Features:

  • Tactile botanical illustrations with raised textures
  • Grade 2 braille descriptions for each plant species
  • Durable, weather-resistant materials for outdoor use
  • Modular design allowing for easy updates and additions

Testing Results: User feedback showed 95% improvement in plant identification accuracy and significantly enhanced visitor engagement.

Enhanced Signage System

Prototype Features:

  • Dual-format information (visual and braille)
  • QR code integration for audio descriptions
  • High-contrast design for low vision accessibility
  • Standardized placement height and positioning

Testing Results: Prototype testing revealed optimal placement at 1.2m height with 30-degree tilt for maximum accessibility.

Digital Integration Prototypes

Accessible Web Platform

Prototype Features:

  • WCAG 2.1 AA compliance for screen readers
  • Voice navigation and audio plant descriptions
  • High contrast mode and adjustable font sizes
  • Mobile-responsive design for on-site use

Testing Results: Accessibility testing with NVDA and JAWS screen readers showed 100% navigation success rate.

User Testing & Validation

Testing Methodology

Our prototype testing involved collaboration with the Royal National Institute of Blind People (RNIB) Scotland and local visually impaired community groups. We conducted multiple rounds of user testing to validate our solutions.

Phase 1: Initial Concept Testing

Tested basic braille integration concepts with 12 visually impaired participants

Phase 2: Prototype Refinement

Iterated designs based on feedback, focusing on tactile quality and information hierarchy

Phase 3: Field Testing

On-site testing at Glasgow Botanic Gardens with real-world conditions and scenarios

Key Findings

  • Braille Quality: Grade 2 braille preferred over Grade 1 for experienced users
  • Material Durability: Weather-resistant materials essential for outdoor installations
  • Information Density: Optimal content length identified as 50-75 words per plant description
  • Digital Integration: QR codes most effective when paired with tactile indicators
  • Navigation: Clear pathways and consistent signage placement crucial for independence
