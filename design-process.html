Design Process & Methodology

Our design process followed a human-centered approach, prioritizing the needs and experiences of visually impaired visitors. Through extensive research, collaboration, and iterative design, we developed comprehensive accessibility solutions for Glasgow Botanic Gardens.

Research & Discovery Phase

Site Analysis & Context Research

Research Activities:

  • Comprehensive site mapping and accessibility audit
  • Analysis of existing signage and information systems
  • Identification of key botanical areas and visitor pathways
  • Documentation of current accessibility challenges

Key Insights: The gardens lacked tactile information systems and had inconsistent signage placement, creating barriers for visually impaired visitors.

Stakeholder Engagement

Research Activities:

  • Interviews with visually impaired community members
  • Consultation with RNIB Scotland accessibility experts
  • Discussions with Glasgow Botanic Gardens staff
  • Survey of current visitor experiences and needs

Key Insights: Users emphasized the importance of independence, detailed plant information, and consistent navigation systems.

Ideation & Concept Development

Collaborative Brainstorming

Design Activities:

  • Multi-disciplinary team brainstorming sessions
  • Accessibility constraint mapping and opportunity identification
  • Concept sketching and rapid ideation workshops
  • Feasibility analysis of proposed solutions

Outcome: Generated 25+ initial concepts, narrowed to 5 core solution areas through evaluation criteria.

Concept Refinement

Design Activities:

  • Detailed concept development and specification
  • Technical feasibility assessment
  • Cost-benefit analysis for implementation
  • Integration planning with existing garden infrastructure

Outcome: Refined concepts into three core solution pillars: braille integration, digital accessibility, and enhanced signage.

Design Methodology Framework

Human-Centered Design Approach

Our design process was grounded in human-centered design principles, ensuring that solutions were developed with and for the visually impaired community rather than simply for them.

Design Challenges & Solutions

Challenge: Testing Constraints and Accessibility Limitations

Problem: Prototype testing required testing with blind and deaf individuals.

Solution: Adapted our testing approach based on our experience on a tour of the Botanics and existing accessibility solutions.

Challenge: Digital Integration

Problem: Ensuring seamless integration between physical braille elements and digital accessibility features.

Solution: Developed unified design language and consistent interaction patterns across all touchpoints.

Challenge: Cost Efficiency

Problem: Creating systems that garden staff could easily maintain and update without specialized training.

Solution: Designed modular components with clear maintenance protocols and staff training materials.
